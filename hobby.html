Building AI Agents

One thing I really enjoy outside of coursework is building AI agents. I find it fun to pick a real-world problem and try to make an agent that can handle it on its own — it's kind of like giving a robot a to-do list and seeing if it can figure things out. Sometimes they work perfectly. Sometimes they go completely off track. But that's what makes it interesting — debugging agent behaviour feels like solving a puzzle.

Most of my experiments start the same way: I spot something repetitive in my daily routine and think "can I get an agent to do this for me?" From there I'll wire up some APIs, add an LLM for reasoning, and see what happens. The tools I reach for most often are:

🔗

LangChain & LangGraph

I mostly use LangChain's LCEL syntax to build ReAct agents with custom tool-calling. For multi-step workflows I've moved to LangGraph — defining nodes as functions, edges as conditions, and letting the graph decide which tool to call next based on the LLM's output. I've also spent a lot of time tuning prompt templates and output parsers to reduce hallucination in structured responses, especially when the agent needs to return valid JSON for downstream processing.

⚙

n8n & Workflow Orchestration

I use n8n when I need to connect external services without writing glue code — things like pulling data from a REST API on a cron schedule, running it through a webhook, and pushing results to Slack or Telegram. Where it gets interesting is combining n8n triggers with Python scripts for heavier logic — like calling an LLM endpoint for summarisation before the final output step.

🗃

RAG & Vector Databases

I've worked with both ChromaDB and FAISS for embedding storage. The real challenge isn't just indexing documents — it's chunking strategy (recursive vs. semantic splitting), picking the right embedding model (I usually compare OpenAI's ada-002 against open-source alternatives), and handling retrieval quality with metadata filters or NVIDIA Reranker as a second pass when top-k results aren't precise enough.

🌐

Web Scraping & Data Acquisition

I use BeautifulSoup with requests for static pages, and Playwright or Selenium for JavaScript-rendered content that needs interaction. For larger jobs I've used Scrapy with custom middlewares for rotation and rate limiting. The trickiest part is always handling anti-bot patterns — dynamic class names, lazy-loaded content, and CAPTCHAs that break your pipeline at 2 AM.

What I enjoy most about building agents is that every failure teaches you something new about how LLMs think, how tools break, and how to design systems that can recover gracefully. It's the closest thing to teaching a machine common sense.

From code to the world

Travelling

Exploring the world, one city at a time.

When I need a break from the screen, I like to travel. I've been lucky enough to visit some amazing places across Asia and Europe. Whether it's walking along the Seine in Paris, exploring temples in Japan, or just finding good street food in a random alley, travelling gives me a fresh perspective. Honestly some of my best project ideas come to me when I'm exploring somewhere new.

Europe

Arc de Triomphe Paris, France Standing at the top and looking down the Champs-Élysées at sunset was one of those moments I'll never forget.
Le Triomphe Paris, France The level of detail carved into this sculpture is insane — every figure tells part of a story about French history.
Disneyland Paris, France Yes, I'm an adult who loves Disneyland. The parade at night with all the lights was absolutely magical.
Tower Bridge London, UK Walked across this bridge at dawn with barely anyone around. London in the early morning hits different.

Asia

Cape Manzamo Okinawa, Japan The water here is unbelievably clear. I spent an entire afternoon just sitting on the cliff watching the waves.
Traditional Garden Kobe, Japan There's a quietness to Japanese gardens that forces you to slow down. Best place to think clearly.
Ise Old Town Mie, Japan Walking through streets that haven't changed in centuries. The mochi here was the best I've ever had.

"There's something special about being somewhere completely different from home — it resets the way I think about problems and usually I come back with a bunch of new ideas."

What Travel Taught Me

Seeing how different cultures solve everyday problems — from Tokyo's train system to London's public signage — changed how I approach UX and system design. Simple solutions that just work are always the hardest to build.

Navigating a city where I don't speak the language forced me to think creatively. That same resourcefulness carries into coding — when the obvious solution won't work, I've learned to look at the problem from a completely different angle.

Getting lost in Osaka at midnight with a dead phone taught me patience like no production bug ever could. Travel and debugging share the same truth: stay calm, break the problem into smaller pieces, and keep moving forward.
